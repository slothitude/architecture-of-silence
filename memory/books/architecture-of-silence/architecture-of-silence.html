The Architecture of Silence

Aaron King

A family that fled persecution built the licensing frameworks of England, then enforced the colonial order in Australia, then found themselves inside the architecture they had created - until one of them finally named it.

Chapter One

The Massacre

Status: Not The Hand

Now

The deck of the Indonesian fishing vessel smells of fish and diesel and something else I cannot name. I am twenty years old. Full boarding kit. The Timor Sea spreads in every direction, blue-grey under a sky that holds no reference points.

The dead man lies in the bow. His face is turned away from me. Around him, hundreds of trepang - sea cucumbers - are piled in rough mounds. They look like nothing I have seen before. Soft. Dark. Waiting for something.

A sailor says something in Bahasa. I do not understand. He points at the dead man, then shrugs. Another sailor translates.

"He old. He sick. They have probably been doing this for hundreds of years."

"Doing what?"

"Fishing. Trepang. To China. That is what the MOU is. Traditional fishing. Next time don't fall asleep."

I look at the dead man. I look at the trepang. I look at the sailor who has just told me that dying on a boat in the Timor Sea is routine, is tradition, is what they have done for hundreds of years and will keep doing.

Then I see the map.

It is pinned to a bulkhead, a navigational chart. But it shows only the northern coast of Australia. Just the top. The Gulf of Carpentaria. The Arafura Sea. The Torres Strait. Everything below the Tropic of Capricorn is white space. Empty.

The part of Australia that existed before Australia.

I stand on the deck and I do not know what I am looking at. I do not know that I will still be looking at it thirty years later. I do not know that the dead man and the trepang and the map showing only the north will become the first page of something I have not started writing yet.

I am twenty years old. I have both my hands. The antenna is five years in my future. The certificate for a trade that does not exist is five years away. The 3am research sessions in Cairns are two decades away.

Right now, I am just a Navy Electronics Technician standing on a fishing boat at Ashmore Reef, looking at a dead man and a map that shows only half a continent, and I do not understand yet why I cannot look away.

Then

France, August 1572.

The heat is the first thing. August in Paris, the air thick with summer and the smell of the Seine and something else - something metallic, something that will not wash out.

Gideon is seven years old.

He does not know what is happening. He knows only that his father has gathered what can be carried, that his mother is silent in a way he has never seen, that the sounds from the street are not normal sounds.

William De Laune is a physician and a minister of the Reformed Church. He has spent eight years studying medicine in Paris and Montpellier. He has published theological works. He has married the daughter of another Reformed minister. He has built a life.

And now he is running.

The St Bartholomew's Day Massacre begins on the night of 23-24 August. The bells of Saint-Germain l'Auxerrois ring out across Paris, and the killing begins. Three thousand Protestants murdered in the city in one night. Seventy thousand across France in the weeks that follow.

Men who refuse to convert to Catholicism are sent to the galleys as slaves. Women are locked up. Children are taken to convents to be raised in the true faith.

This is what it looks like when the state removes the frameworks that protect you.

Gideon does not see the worst of it. William gets him out. They flee to the coast - to La Rochelle perhaps, or to Nantes, to any port with ships leaving for England. The journey takes days or weeks. The boy sees things he will never speak of. The father carries the weight of what they have lost.

They land at Rye, in East Sussex.

Rye is the largest port in Sussex in the late sixteenth century. By 1582, more than half the population - some fifteen hundred people - will be Huguenots. Refugees. People exactly like the De Laune family.

William De Laune arrives in England with nothing but his training, his faith, and his seven-year-old son.

He does not know that Gideon will live to ninety-four. He does not know that Gideon will become apothecary to a queen, will found a society that still exists four hundred years later, will leave an estate worth ninety-three thousand pounds. He does not know that the family will acquire a coat of arms, a manor in Kent, property in Virginia and Bermuda.

He does not know that the persecuted will become the enforcers.

Right now, William De Laune is just a refugee standing on a dock in Rye, looking at a country that may or may not take him in, holding the hand of a boy who has seen things no child should see.

now

Now

I did not know any of this when I stood on that fishing boat.

I did not know that my ancestor William De Laune had fled France four hundred and twenty-three years before I was born. I did not know that he had been a physician with eight years of training who arrived in England with nothing. I did not know that his son Gideon would build something extraordinary from that nothing.

I did not know that the family would become the establishment - magistrates, physicians to prime ministers, men with streets named after them.

And I did not know that I would be the first one in four hundred years to end up on the wrong side of what they built.

The dead man on the boat is not my ancestor. He is not my story. But he is where my story starts, because standing over him at twenty years old, looking at the trepang and the map showing only the north, something happened that I cannot explain.

A question formed that I did not know how to ask yet.

Why does the map show only the top?

Why do they say nothing happened here?

Why is the dead man routine?

The sailor said: Next time don't fall asleep.

I have never forgotten that. Not in twenty years of Navy service. Not through certificates for trades that don't exist. Not through both hands on a live antenna. Not through 3am research sessions in Cairns that pulled the thread all the way back to France in 1572.

Next time don't fall asleep.

He was not talking about me. He was talking about the dead man. The old man who got sick and died on a boat doing what his people had done for hundreds of years.

But I heard it differently.

I heard: Pay attention. This matters. The map that shows only half a continent matters. The dead man surrounded by trepang matters. The fact that this is routine matters.

I did not fall asleep.

I am still awake.

Then

William De Laune settles in Blackfriars, London, by 1575.

The Blackfriars district is a Liberty - a zone with unique freedoms inherited from the old Dominican friary. Freedom from arrest by City officials. Exemption from City taxes. Freedom to practice crafts and trades without guild memberships.

This last freedom is crucial for a foreign physician with no English licence.

The parish is a Puritan stronghold. Mixed population: aristocratic Englishmen, skilled foreign artisans, a large French Protestant community. It is safe. It is not entirely English, but it is not hostile either.

William resumes his practice. He is both physician and minister - the Reformed Church has no objection to dual vocations. He preaches. He heals. He raises his son.

Gideon grows up in Blackfriars. He learns his father's trade - the herbs, the remedies, the knowledge passed from physician to son. He does not go to university. He does not serve an English apprenticeship. He learns by watching, by doing, by absorbing everything his father knows.

In 1582, when Gideon is seventeen, William De Laune is hauled before the College of Physicians.

The charge is simple: practising medicine without an English licence.

William has eight years of training from the finest medical schools in Europe. He has qualifications that would give him standing in any court in France. But in England, he has no standing. No licence. No recognition.

The College of Physicians is the guild that controls medical practice in London. They protect their members from competition. And William De Laune - Huguenot refugee, foreign-trained physician - is competition.

The outcome of the case is not recorded.

But we know what happened next. William did not stop practising. Gideon did not stop learning. The family did not give up.

They found another way.

Now

Four hundred and thirty-three years later, I am standing in a Navy workshop looking at a certificate that means nothing.

Certificate III in Electronics. Certificate IV in Electronics. Six years of training. Service records that show I have done the work. Qualifications that would give me standing in any other Five Eyes country.

But Australia has no Electronics Technician licence.

I look at training.gov.au. There is no licensing framework. No regulatory body. No standing.

I ask around. The answer is always the same: the Navy certificate should be enough. Civilian employers will recognise it.

But civilian employers require a defence security clearance for electronics work. And to get a defence security clearance, you need to be sponsored by an employer. And to get sponsored by an employer, you need to already have the clearance.

The circular trap.

I am not William De Laune standing before the College of Physicians. I am not a refugee with no English licence. I am an Australian citizen, born in Brisbane, trained by the Royal Australian Navy.

And yet.

Four hundred and thirty-three years later, I have the same problem my ancestor had.

No standing.

The framework that would give me standing does not exist.

And I do not know yet that the reason it does not exist is the same reason the map on that fishing boat showed only the northern coast of Australia.

I do not know that the absence of frameworks is not random.

I do not know that it is architecture.

Then

The seven-year-old boy who fled the St Bartholomew's Day Massacre does not know what he will become.

He does not know that at age forty-five he will be appointed apothecary to Anne of Denmark, Queen of James I. He does not know that at age fifty-two he will work with Francis Bacon to found the Society of Apothecaries. He does not know that at age seventy-two he will serve as Master of that Society. He does not know that at age ninety-four he will die leaving an estate worth ninety-three thousand pounds, a white marble statue in Apothecaries' Hall, and a family that has become part of the English establishment.

He does not know that his father's humiliation before the College of Physicians will be answered by his own creation of a licensing body that gives standing to medical practitioners across England and Wales.

He does not know that the persecuted will become the licencers.

Right now, Gideon De Laune is just a boy on a ship crossing the Channel, leaving behind everything his family has known, heading toward a country that may or may not want them.

He carries with him the trauma of what he has seen. The sounds from the streets of Paris. The smell of blood in summer heat. The faces of people who were neighbours one day and murderers the next.

He will carry it for the rest of his ninety-four years.

He will be "noted for many singularities" - a phrase that will appear in the records, that will puzzle historians, that will point toward something not quite ordinary about this man who built so much from so little.

But that is all in the future.

Right now, Gideon is seven years old, and the Channel stretches before him, and England is a rumour of safety, and his father's hand is tight on his, and the dead are behind him, and the living are not yet done with their building.

Chapter Two

The Licence

Status: Not The Hand

Now

HMAS Benalla, 2015.

Both hands on a live antenna.

I am forty years old. I have spent six years in the Royal Australian Navy as an Electronics Technician. I hold Certificate III and Certificate IV. I have done the work. I have the training. I have the service record.

The antenna is live because the standard is wrong. The distance is wrong. Someone - I do not know who - has connected something incorrectly, and now I am standing in a compartment with current running through metal that I am holding with both hands.

I do not let go.

This is not bravery. This is training. You do not let go. You wait for someone to isolate the circuit. You wait for the current to stop. You do not move.

The seconds pass. The silence stretches.

Someone finds the breaker. The current stops. I let go.

My hands are shaking. Not much. Just enough that I notice.

I am forty years old, and I have just had both hands on a live antenna, and I do not understand yet that this is the moment everything changes.

---

In the months after, I start asking questions.

Why was the standard wrong?

Why was the distance wrong?

Why am I - a trained Electronics Technician with six years of service - unable to fix a civilian television?

I look at my certificates. Certificate III in Electronics. Certificate IV in Electronics. Navy qualifications that should translate to civilian work.

I search training.gov.au - the national register of vocational education and training in Australia.

There is no Electronics Technician licence.

There is no regulatory framework.

There is no licensing requirement.

I read the words on the screen, and I do not understand them. Every other trade has a licence. Electricians. Plumbers. Gasfitters. Mechanics. Even hairdressers have regulatory frameworks.

But Electronics Technicians do not.

I call the union. They confirm it. I call training organisations. They confirm it. I look at job advertisements - civilian employers require a defence security clearance for electronics work.

And to get a defence security clearance, you need to be sponsored by an employer.

And to get sponsored by an employer, you need to already have the clearance.

The circular trap.

I am standing in the same place William De Laune stood in 1582.

Eight years of training. A degree from Europe's finest medical school. No standing in England.

No licence. No framework. No recognition.

Four hundred and thirty-three years apart.

The same architecture.

Then

London, 1582.

William De Laune stands before the College of Physicians.

He is fifty-two years old. He has spent eight years studying medicine in Paris and Montpellier - the finest medical education available in Europe. He is a qualified physician. He is also an ordained minister of the Reformed Church. He has published theological works. He has built a life in England after fleeing the massacre that killed seventy thousand of his fellow Protestants.

And now he is being accused of practising medicine without a licence.

The College of Physicians is a guild. Its purpose is to protect the interests of its members - English physicians who have trained in England, who serve English patients, who pay English fees. William De Laune is not English. He was not trained in England. He does not have an English licence.

The fact that his training is better than most English physicians is irrelevant. The fact that he fled religious persecution is irrelevant. The fact that he has saved lives in the Blackfriars district for years is irrelevant.

What matters is standing.

And William De Laune has none.

---

The case is recorded, but the outcome is not.

We do not know if he was fined. We do not know if he was barred from practice. We do not know if he simply ignored the College and continued his work.

What we know is this: William De Laune did not stop practising medicine. His son Gideon did not stop learning. The family did not give up.

And twenty-five years later, Gideon De Laune would help create the very framework that had been used against his father.

now

Now

I do not know any of this in 2015.

I do not know that my ancestor stood before the College of Physicians with the same problem I have. I do not know that the framework I am looking for - the Electronics Technician licence that should exist - was the kind of framework my ancestor's son would help create in 1617.

I am just a forty-year-old Navy veteran standing in a workshop, holding certificates that mean nothing, looking at a screen that tells me there is no framework to give me standing.

And I am angry.

Not because I cannot fix televisions. Not because the job market is closed. Not because I have to start over.

I am angry because something is wrong.

The absence of a framework is not an accident. The circular trap is not a mistake. The fact that Australia is the only Five Eyes country without an Electronics Technician licence is not coincidence.

But I do not know that yet.

All I know is that I have both hands on a live antenna and the current has stopped and I am still shaking and something in me has started to pull at a thread that will not stop unravelling.

Then

What did it feel like to be William De Laune in 1582?

To have fled France with nothing. To have rebuilt a life in a foreign country. To have learned a new language, found new patients, established a new practice. To have survived.

And then to stand before a guild of English physicians who tell you that your eight years of training mean nothing. That your degree from Montpellier means nothing. That your standing in France means nothing.

To be told: You have no licence. You have no standing. You are not one of us.

William had spent his entire adult life learning to heal. And now a guild of men who had learned the same things in different places claimed the right to decide who could practice and who could not.

This is what the architecture looks like from the outside.

Not dramatic. Not violent. Just the quiet assertion of power by those who have it over those who do not.

William De Laune was NOT THE HAND.

He was subject to the system. Outside it. Without standing.

And his son Gideon would spend his life making sure his family never stood outside again.

now

Now

I do not think about ancestors in 2015.

I think about the antenna. The wrong standard. The wrong distance. The silence after the current stopped.

I think about the certificates on my wall that mean nothing to civilian employers. The framework that should exist but does not. The standing I should have but cannot find.

I do not know yet that the question forming in me - why is there no licence? - will pull me back through four hundred years of family history. I do not know that I will find William De Laune standing before the College of Physicians. I do not know that I will find Gideon De Laune building the framework his father lacked.

All I know is this: something is wrong. The absence is not random. The gap is not a mistake.

And I am going to find out why.

Then

William De Laune did not give up.

He continued to practise. He continued to teach his son. He continued to build something from the nothing he had arrived with.

And in 1610, his son Gideon was appointed apothecary to Anne of Denmark, Queen of James I.

The refugee's son. The boy who had fled the massacre at seven years old. The young man who had learned his trade not in university but in his father's shop.

Now he served a queen.

The framework that had excluded his father - the guild system, the licensing requirements, the standing that came only from English training - Gideon would spend his life changing it.

Not by fighting against it.

By becoming part of it.

By building something new within it.

By transforming NOT THE HAND into THE HAND.

But that is the next chapter.

---

now

---

Now

I am forty years old.

I have both hands on a live antenna.

The current has stopped.

And the question that will take me four hundred years into my family's past is just beginning to form.

Why is there no licence?

Why is there no framework?

Why do I have no standing?

William De Laune asked the same questions in 1582.

His son answered them in 1617.

I will not find the answer for another decade.

But the thread has been pulled.

And it will not stop unravelling until it reaches all the way back to France, to August 1572, to a seven-year-old boy watching his father gather what can be carried while outside the killing begins.

Chapter Three

The Builder

Status: Becoming The Hand

Now

3am, Cairns, 2024.

The screen glows in the dark room. I have been researching for hours. The thread I started pulling on that fishing boat at Ashmore Reef has led me here - to a name, a date, a charter.

Gideon De Laune. 1617. The Society of Apothecaries.

I read the words on the screen. My ancestor - the seven-year-old boy who fled the St Bartholomew's Day Massacre - grew up to become apothecary to the Queen of England. He worked with Francis Bacon. He helped found a society that still exists today. He built the licensing framework for medicine in England.

The family that had NO STANDING built the framework that GIVES standing.

NOT THE HAND became THE HAND.

I sit in the darkness and I try to understand what this means. My ancestor was hauled before the College of Physicians for practising without a licence. Twenty-five years later, his son helped create the framework that would licence medical practitioners across England and Wales.

The persecuted became the licencers.

And four hundred years later, I am standing in the same place William De Laune stood - without a licence, without a framework, without standing.

Except there is a difference.

William De Laune faced the College of Physicians. I face nothing. No college. No framework. No licensing body at all.

The framework Gideon built does not exist for Electronics Technicians.

The architecture his family helped create in England was never built in Australia.

Epilogue

Status: Naming It

Now

It is still 3am.

Or maybe it is a different 3am. A later one. After the research. After the writing. After the naming.

The thread has been pulled. The architecture has been named. The silence has been broken.

---

I sit in the darkness and I think about what comes next.

Then

Four hundred years ago, a seven-year-old boy fled the St Bartholomew's Day Massacre.

He arrived in England with nothing. No standing. No recognition. No framework to protect him.

He grew up. He learned a trade. He built a framework that would give standing to people like himself.

The refugee became the licencer. NOT THE HAND became THE HAND.

now

---

Now

I am descended from that boy.

I carry his blood. I carry the knowledge of how architecture works. I carry the understanding of what frameworks do and what their absence means.

But I am not him.

I cannot build a licensing framework by myself. I cannot force the government to recognise my trade. I cannot create the architecture that should exist.

---

Or maybe I can.

Not by building. Not by forcing. But by naming.

---

then

---

Then

The naming is the beginning.

Not the end. Not the solution. Not the framework.

But the beginning of visibility. Of understanding. Of the ability to change.

The architecture of silence cannot survive being named.

Now

I think about my grandmother.

Elizabeth Yvonne Faunce. The Queensland branch. NOT THE HAND.

She knew the stories. She passed them down. She gave me the thread without knowing what it would lead to.

She could not name the architecture. She could not see the pattern. But she preserved the knowledge that made this book possible.

---

I am the first one in four hundred years to name what my ancestors built.

Not because I am special. Not because I am smarter or more capable.

Because I am the one who fell into the gap. The one who asked the question. The one who pulled the thread.

The thread is still pulling.

From 1572 to now. From France to England to Australia. From persecution to framework-building to enforcement to absence to naming.

The pattern continues. The architecture persists.

But now it is visible.

What happens next is not up to me.

The architecture of silence will continue to operate. The gap will continue to swallow Defence Force personnel. The absence of frameworks will continue to exclude skilled workers.

But the architecture has been named.

And naming is the beginning of change.
