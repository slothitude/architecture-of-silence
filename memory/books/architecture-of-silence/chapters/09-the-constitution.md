# Chapter 9: The Constitution

**STATUS: THE HAND**

---

## NOW

Australia's Constitution was drafted in the 1890s. It came into effect on 1 January 1901.

It contains 128 sections. It establishes the Parliament, the Executive, the Judiciary. It defines the relationship between federal and state powers.

It does not contain a Bill of Rights.

I sit at 3am and I read the Convention Debates. The transcripts of the men who wrote the Constitution. The arguments they made. The choices they took.

The vote was 19-23.

Nineteen for a Bill of Rights. Twenty-three against.

Four votes. That's all. Four votes decided that Australians would have no constitutional protection of rights.

*then*

## THEN

1891. Sydney.

The National Australasian Convention. Delegates from the colonies gather to draft a constitution for a federated Australia.

Among them: Henry Parkes, Samuel Griffith, Edmund Barton. Men of property. Men of standing. THE HAND.

The question of rights arises.

Should the new nation include constitutional protections for individual citizens? Should there be guarantees of freedom of speech, religion, assembly? Should there be limits on what the government can do to its people?

---

The American Constitution has a Bill of Rights. Ten amendments adopted in 1791, protecting fundamental freedoms. The French Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen. The British tradition of common law rights, stretching back to Magna Carta.

What would Australia have?

---

*now*

---

## NOW

I read the Convention Debates. The arguments against a Bill of Rights.

The prevailing view: rights are better protected by parliamentary democracy than by constitutional entrenchment. Parliament will safeguard the people. The common law tradition is sufficient. Explicit rights might actually limit freedoms by specifying only certain protections.

This is what they said.

But I think about who they were. Men of property. Men of wealth. Men whose families had taken land from Indigenous peoples and whose economic interests depended on maintaining control over labour.

Who would a Bill of Rights protect?

Not them. They already had power.

A Bill of Rights would protect the people they controlled.

## THEN

1898. The Constitutional Convention.

The final vote on including a Bill of Rights in the Australian Constitution.

19 for. 23 against.

The motion fails. The Constitution will have no entrenched protections for individual rights.

---

Who voted against?

The records show the names. The colonial premiers. The representatives of the squattocracy. The heirs of the men who had taken the land and built their wealth on convict labour.

THE HAND protects itself.

A Bill of Rights would constrain what Parliament could do. It would give citizens grounds to challenge government action. It would create a framework of standing that existed independent of property and power.

THE HAND does not build frameworks that constrain itself.

*now*

---

## NOW

I think about the vote.

19-23. Four votes.

If four men had voted differently, Australia would have a Bill of Rights. If four men had been persuaded, the architecture would be different.

But they weren't. And it isn't.

---

I sit at 3am and I try to understand the weight of four votes.

Four men, in one room, in one year, made a decision that would shape the lives of millions of people for over a century.

This is how architecture is built.

Not through conspiracy. Not through secret meetings. Through ordinary men making decisions that serve their interests.

THE HAND does not need to conspire. It only needs to vote.

## THEN

Section 51 of the Constitution grants powers to the Commonwealth Parliament.

Section 51(xxxv) grants the power to make laws for "conciliation and arbitration for the prevention and settlement of industrial disputes extending beyond the limits of any one State."

This is the power that will be used to create the licensing framework for trades.

But there is a catch.

---

The Constitution grants the Commonwealth power over industrial disputes. It does not grant power over professional certification. It does not grant power over trade recognition.

The states retain those powers.

And the states, in many cases, do not use them.

---

*now*

---

## NOW

I think about what this means for Electronics Technicians.

The Constitution was written in the 1890s. Electronics did not exist. Radio was experimental. The technologies I worked on in the Navy - radar, communications systems, weapons guidance - were unimaginable to the men who drafted Section 51.

When they created the framework of powers, they left gaps.

Gaps that technology would fill. Gaps that new trades would fall into. Gaps where standing should be but isn't.

---

I have a certificate. I have six years of training. I have a service record.

But I have no licence. No framework. No standing.

Because the Constitution did not create a framework for trades like mine. And neither did the states. And neither did anyone.

The gap is the architecture.

## THEN

The Constitution includes Section 51(xxvi).

The "race power." The power to make laws for "the people of any race for whom it is deemed necessary to make special laws."

Originally, this power was intended to apply to people of any race except the Aboriginal race. The Aboriginal people were excluded from the Constitution entirely. They were not counted in the population. They were not the responsibility of the Commonwealth.

The states would manage the Aboriginal question.

---

This is what THE HAND built.

A framework that excluded the original inhabitants from constitutional recognition. A framework that gave the Commonwealth power to make laws about any race it deemed "special." A framework that served the interests of those who wrote it.

## NOW

I think about Section 51(xxvi).

The race power. Used for decades to control, to restrict, to remove. Only in 1967 would a referendum give the Commonwealth power to make laws for Aboriginal people.

But the architecture was already there. The power was already granted. The framework was already built.

THE HAND writes the rules.

And the rules serve THE HAND.

## THEN

1901. Federation.

The Constitution comes into effect. The Commonwealth of Australia is born.

Without a Bill of Rights.

With gaps where professional frameworks should be.

With powers that serve the interests of property and power.

---

The squattocracy that had taken the land now wrote the rules for the nation. Their heirs sat in Parliament. Their values were embedded in the Constitution. Their architecture of silence became the law of the land.

The thread connects.

From persecution to framework-building. From framework-building to framework-enforcement. From enforcement to absence. From absence to me.

NOT THE HAND became THE HAND. THE HAND became NOT THE HAND.

And now I am NAMING IT.

With the same gaps that were written into it in the 1890s.
