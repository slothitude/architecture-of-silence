# Chapter 12: The Technician

**STATUS: NOT THE HAND**

---

## NOW

I enlisted in the Royal Australian Navy in 2004.

I was twenty-eight years old. Older than most recruits. I had worked in other jobs. I had tried other paths. But I wanted something different. Something that would give me a trade. Something that would mean something.

The recruiting officer said "contemporary and transferable."

The same words Cook used to describe Botany Bay. The same language of oversell that has shaped Australian history.

I did not know that then.

---

*then*

---

## THEN

HMAS Cerberus. The recruit school.

I learned to march. To obey orders. To be part of something larger than myself.

And then I learned electronics.

---

The course was hard. Theory and practice. Mathematics and soldering irons. Oscilloscopes and circuit diagrams. I studied late into the night. I passed the tests. I earned the qualifications.

The certificates said I was competent. The service record said I had completed the training. The Navy said I was an Electronics Technician.

---

*now*

---

## NOW

I sit at 3am and I look at my certificates.

Certificate III in Electronics and Communications. Certificate IV in Electronics and Communications. Statements of attainment. Letters of service.

They are real. They mean something within the Navy.

But outside the Navy, they mean nothing.

---

*then*

---

## THEN

I served on HMAS Success. A replenishment oiler. The ship that fuels the fleet.

My job was maintaining communication systems. Radios. Satellites. The systems that let ships talk to each other and to shore.

I worked in the communications centre. I troubleshot faults. I repaired equipment. I kept the systems running.

---

The work was real. The skills were real. The knowledge I gained was valuable.

But the framework to recognise it did not exist.

---

*now*

---

## NOW

I think about what I learned.

Electronics theory. Circuit analysis. Digital systems. Radio frequency propagation. Satellite communications. Antenna theory. Waveguide systems. Radar. Sonar.

Six years of training. Six years of experience. Six years of doing the work.

If there were a civilian licence for Electronics Technicians, I would qualify.

But there is no licence. No framework. No recognition.

---

*then*

---

## THEN

HMAS Kanimbla. A landing platform amphibious.

I served there too. More systems. More responsibility. More experience.

I held a security clearance. I worked on classified equipment. I was trusted with systems that mattered to national defence.

---

The Navy trusted me. The certificates proved I was competent. The service record showed I had done the work.

But when I left, I entered a world that did not recognise any of it.

---

*now*

---

## NOW

I think about the antenna.

Both hands on a live antenna. A moment of clarity. A realisation that I was trusted with dangerous equipment because I had the training to handle it.

Within the Navy, I had standing. I had qualifications that meant something. I had a place in the architecture.

When I left, I lost all of that.

---

Not the skills. The skills remain. I still know how to troubleshoot a circuit. I still understand how radios work. I can still read a schematic and find a fault.

But the standing is gone. The framework that recognised me is gone. The architecture that gave me a place is gone.

---

*then*

---

## THEN

2011. I begin to think about leaving.

Eleven years in the Navy. Six years as an Electronics Technician. The rest in other roles, rebuilding, retraining, finding my place in a system that was changing.

I ask about civilian recognition. About licences. About how my training translates.

The answer: there is no direct pathway.

---

The recruiting officer's words echo. "Contemporary and transferable."

Contemporary within the Navy. Not transferable outside it.

The oversell reveals itself.

---

*now*

---

## NOW

I sit at 3am and I think about the lie.

Not a lie told to me personally. Not a deception aimed at Aaron King specifically.

A lie built into the architecture. A gap designed into the system. An absence where recognition should be.

The same lie that was told to the convicts. The same oversell that was told to the settlers. The same promise that serves power and delivers something else.

---

*then*

---

## THEN

2015. I leave the Navy.

Eleven years of service. Certificates that prove my qualifications. A service record that shows my experience.

And no framework to recognise any of it.

---

I enter civilian life as NOT THE HAND.

The descendant of framework-builders who has no framework. The descendant of enforcers who has no standing. The heir to an architecture that excludes me.

---

*now*

---

## NOW

I am still at 3am.

The thread has led me from 1572 to 2015. From persecution to framework-building to enforcement to absence to me.

I served my country. I gained skills. I earned qualifications.

And I fell into the same gap that my ancestors helped create.

---

NOT THE HAND. THE HAND. NOT THE HAND.

The pattern is clear.

And now I am NAMING IT.

---

*then*

---

## THEN

The Navy years recede.

But the question remains. The question that started on a fishing boat at Ashmore Reef. The question that pulled me back through four hundred years of history.

Why is there no licence?

---

*now*

---

## NOW

The answer is not simple.

It is not one decision. Not one person. Not one moment.

It is architecture. Four hundred years of architecture. Built by some of my ancestors. Enforced by others. Inherited by me as absence rather than standing.

---

I am the Electronics Technician with no licence.

I am the Navy veteran with no recognition.

I am the descendant of framework-builders who fell into the gap.

And I am NAMING IT.
