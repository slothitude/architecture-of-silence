# Chapter 4: The Singularities

**STATUS: THE HAND**

---

## NOW

The phrase appears in the records like a splinter under the skin.

"A man noted for many singularities in his time."

Thomas Delaune wrote it in 1681, describing Gideon De Laune. Twenty-two years after Gideon's death. A tribute to the founder of the Society of Apothecaries, the man who built the licensing framework for medicine in England.

Singularities.

I look up the word. In seventeenth-century English, it means "the state of being singular, distinct, peculiar, uncommon or unusual." An unusual action or behaviour. Something remarkable. Something not quite ordinary.

What were Gideon's singularities?

The records do not say. They note only that he had them. That he was known for them. That people remembered them even after his death.

---

I sit at 3am and I think about what it means to be "noted for many singularities."

It is not the same as being praised. It is not the same as being honoured. It is something stranger - a recognition that this man was not like other men. That he carried something unusual. That there was more to him than the public records show.

What had Gideon seen that shaped him?

The St Bartholomew's Day Massacre. The flight from France. The arrival in England with nothing. His father's humiliation before the College of Physicians. The decades of building - the Society, the pills, the fortune, the properties in three countries.

A man who fled persecution at seven years old and died at ninety-four having outlived almost everyone he knew.

What singularities would such a man carry?

We do not know. The records preserve only the outline.

But we know this: the refugee's son became THE HAND. The man without standing built the framework that gives standing. The boy who watched his father shamed before the College of Physicians created the Society that would licence practitioners across England.

And when he died, his white marble statue was placed in Apothecaries' Hall, where it still stands today.

## THEN

1659.

Gideon De Laune dies at ninety-four.

His estate is valued at ninety-three thousand pounds.

In modern terms, this is millions. A fortune built from pills - from a secret formula that cured scurvy, jaundice, venereal disease, worms, dropsy. A remedy that others tried to copy and failed. A preparation that entered the pharmacopoeia as "Pilula ex Duobus" - the pill of two ingredients.

Mainly colocynth and scammony.

But prepared in a way that no one else could duplicate.

---

What was in Gideon's pills?

We know the official ingredients. Colocynth - a bitter apple, a drastic purgative. Scammony - a convolvulus root, also a purgative. Two ingredients. Hence the name.

But preparation matters. The way herbs are dried, ground, combined, formed. The proportions. The timing. The knowledge that passes from father to son, from master to apprentice, through demonstration rather than text.

Gideon learned his trade from his father William. He spent eighty-seven years in England practising it. He knew things that others did not.

This is what it means to be "noted for many singularities."

Not magic. Not alchemy - though alchemy was in the air in 1617, when Gideon helped found the Society of Apothecaries at the height of the Rosicrucian furore. Not supernatural.

Just knowledge. Deep, specific, practical knowledge. The kind that comes from a lifetime of attention to detail. The kind that lets a refugee's son compound remedies that others cannot.

The pills made Gideon wealthy. But the knowledge made him powerful.

*now*

## NOW

I think about secret formulas.

Gideon's pills. De Laune's Pills. A remedy that made a fortune, that was copied by quacks, that entered the official pharmacopoeia.

The formula was not written down - or if it was, the writing has been lost. What remains is the fact of it: a preparation that worked, that sold, that built an estate worth ninety-three thousand pounds.

What kind of knowledge is worth that much?

In the seventeenth century, the answer is: medical knowledge. The ability to cure diseases that killed. The skill to compound remedies that worked when others failed.

In the twenty-first century, the answer is different. Knowledge is everywhere. Training is standardised. The internet puts the sum of human learning at anyone's fingertips.

And yet.

I have six years of Navy training in electronics. Certificates that prove my skill. A service record that shows I have done the work.

And no framework exists to recognise it.

The knowledge is there. The standing is not.

## THEN

Gideon's properties at his death:

An estate at Roxton, Bedfordshire.

The manor of Sharsted, Kent.

A mansion in Blackfriars, London.

Extensive property in Virginia and Bermuda.

The man who arrived in England at seven years old with nothing now owned land in three countries. The refugee had become a property owner. The persecuted had become the establishment.

THE HAND holds wealth. Holds land. Holds power.

---

Sharsted Court in Kent becomes the family seat. Gideon purchased it for his son Abraham around 1625. It will remain in the family for three hundred years, passing through generations of De Launes, then through marriage to the Faunce family, until it is finally sold in 1966.

The property in Virginia and Bermuda - what became of it?

The records do not say. Gideon owned land in the American colonies at a time when those colonies were just beginning. When the Pilgrims had barely landed. When the architecture of the New World was still being formed.

Did Gideon ever think about his American properties? Did he imagine what they might become? Did he consider what it meant for a refugee from France to own land taken from indigenous peoples in a new continent?

We do not know. The records preserve only the fact of ownership.

But we can imagine.

A man who fled persecution. A man who built wealth from knowledge. A man who acquired property in three countries as insurance against the fragility of any single nation.

Gideon never forgot what happened in France in 1572.

He never forgot what it meant to have no standing.

And he spent ninety-four years making sure his family would never be vulnerable again.

*now*

## NOW

I think about what it means to own property in three countries.

Gideon De Laune was a refugee. He arrived in England with nothing. He built a fortune from pills. And then he diversified - land in England, land in the American colonies.

Insurance. Against what?

Against everything.

Against religious persecution, which he had already survived. Against political instability, which he had watched tear England apart during the Civil War. Against the fragility of any single nation, any single system.

Gideon understood architecture.

Not just the architecture of licensing frameworks, but the architecture of survival. Spread the risk. Own property in multiple jurisdictions. Build wealth that cannot be taken away by any single catastrophe.

This is what THE HAND does.

It builds structures that protect against the chaos of history. It creates frameworks that give standing regardless of circumstance. It acquires resources that cannot be easily destroyed.

---

I sit at 3am and I think about my own situation.

I have no property in three countries. I have no secret formula that generates wealth. I have no marble statue in a livery hall.

What I have is a certificate for a trade that does not exist. A Navy service record that means nothing to civilian employers. A question that started on a fishing boat at Ashmore Reef.

But I also have this: knowledge of where I came from. Understanding of how the architecture was built. Awareness that the same bloodline that built the licensing framework in England enforced its absence in Australia.

THE HAND became NOT THE HAND.

And now I am NAMING IT.

## THEN

The white marble statue in Apothecaries' Hall.

Gideon De Laune, sculpted in stone, placed in the hall of the Society he helped found. A permanent presence. A reminder to every apothecary who enters that this man - this refugee's son, this builder of frameworks - created the structure within which they work.

The statue was erected after his death. It stands there still, four hundred years later.

What does it mean to be memorialised in stone?

Not just remembered. Not just honoured. Fixed. Permanent. A presence that outlasts flesh and blood, that persists through centuries of change.

Gideon De Laune died in 1659. But his statue stands in 2024. His Society continues to licence practitioners. His framework - completed in 1815, two hundred years after his death - still controls who can practice medicine in England.

This is what THE HAND builds.

Structures that outlast individuals. Frameworks that persist through generations. Architecture that shapes the world long after the builders are gone.

*now*

## NOW

I have never seen the statue.

I have never been to Apothecaries' Hall. I have never walked the streets of Blackfriars where Gideon lived and worked. I have never stood in the church where he is buried.

But I know it exists.

And knowing changes something.

The architecture is not abstract. It is not just a pattern I have identified in records and archives. It is stone and mortar, framework and standing, the accumulated weight of four hundred years of building.

Gideon built the licensing framework for medicine.

His descendants - my family - carried that knowledge of how architecture works to Australia. And there, instead of building, they enforced absence. Instead of creating frameworks, they maintained gaps. Instead of giving standing, they preserved a system where standing depends on power and power belongs to those who already have it.

THE HAND does not just build.

It also prevents building.

## THEN

Gideon's children.

Seventeen of them. Most died in infancy - a common tragedy in the seventeenth century. Only a few survived to carry the family forward.

Abraham De Laune, the son for whom Sharsted Court was purchased. He married Anne, daughter of Sir Richard Sandys of Northbourne Court, Kent. The refugee's son married into the gentry.

Anne De Laune, a daughter, married Sir Richard Sprignell, baronet, of Coppenthorpe, Yorkshire. Another gentry marriage.

The family that had fled France with nothing now married into the English establishment.

NOT THE HAND had become THE HAND. And THE HAND marries its own.

---

By the time Gideon dies in 1659, the De Laune family is secure. Wealthy. Connected. Part of the system rather than outside it.

The framework that William De Laune lacked - the standing that comes from recognition - his son has not only acquired it. He has helped create the system that grants it.

And his descendants will carry that knowledge of architecture-building forward.

To Australia.

To the Constitution of 1901.

To the design of a nation without a Bill of Rights.

But that is later chapters.

*now*

## NOW

The phrase stays with me.

"A man noted for many singularities in his time."

What were Gideon's singularities?

Perhaps the secret formula for his pills. Perhaps the knowledge that came from eighty-seven years of practice. Perhaps the memory of what it cost to have no standing - and the determination to never be vulnerable again.

Or perhaps something else. Something the records do not capture. Something that passed from father to son to grandson, through blood rather than text.

I think about what singularities I carry.

Both hands on a live antenna. A certificate for a trade that does not exist. A question that started on a fishing boat and pulled me back four hundred years.

Is that a singularity?

Or is it just the same architecture, repeating itself, generation after generation?

NOT THE HAND. THE HAND. NOT THE HAND. NAMING IT.

The pattern is clear. What it means is not.

But I am still pulling the thread.

And the thread leads next to marriage. To the Faunce family. To the joining of two bloodlines that will carry the architecture to Australia.

## THEN

Gideon De Laune is buried under the stone in the porch of St Anne's Church, Blackfriars.

The same district where his father William settled after fleeing France. The same Liberty where refugees could practice trades without guild membership. The same Blackfriars that had given the family a foothold in England.

He had arrived at seven years old with nothing.

He died at ninety-four having built everything.

The framework he helped create would outlast him by centuries. The properties he owned would pass through generations. The statue in his honour would stand in Apothecaries' Hall until the present day.

This is what it looks like when NOT THE HAND becomes THE HAND.

Not through luck. Not through magic. Through construction. Through building. Through the patient accumulation of standing, of wealth, of connections, of frameworks that give the next generation a place within the system rather than outside it.

Gideon De Laune died in 1659.

But the architecture he built is still standing.
