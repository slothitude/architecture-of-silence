# Chapter 10: The Dismissal

**STATUS: THE HAND**

---

## NOW

11 November 1975.

I am not born yet. I will arrive the following year.

But the event that happens on this day will shape the architecture I will navigate as an adult. The decision that is made in Canberra will echo through the decades and land in my lap as a question on a fishing boat at Ashmore Reef.

Gough Whitlam is dismissed.

---

I sit at 3am and I read the cables. The declassified documents. The letters between Canberra and Washington.

The official story: a constitutional crisis. A Prime Minister who could not pass supply. A Governor-General who used his reserve powers to resolve a deadlock.

But there is more.

---

*then*

---

## THEN

1972. Gough Whitlam leads the Labor Party to victory.

"It's time for change." Health care for all. Free university education. Recognition of Aboriginal land rights. Independence in foreign policy.

The architecture trembles.

---

Whitlam's government creates Medibank - the foundation of universal health care. It ends conscription. It withdraws troops from Vietnam. It recognises the People's Republic of China. It passes the Racial Discrimination Act.

It begins to question things that have never been questioned.

---

*now*

---

## NOW

I think about what Whitlam represented.

Not just policy changes. A challenge to the architecture itself.

For seventy-four years, Australia had been governed by the framework created in 1901. The framework that protected property. The framework that excluded rights. The framework that served THE HAND.

Whitlam threatened to change the framework.

---

*then*

---

## THEN

1975. The crisis builds.

The Opposition, led by Malcolm Fraser, blocks supply in the Senate. The government cannot pass its budget. It cannot fund its programs. It runs out of money.

The constitutional crisis deepens.

---

Behind the scenes, other forces are at work.

The CIA. The British government. The Australian intelligence services. The Americans have been concerned about Whitlam since 1972. His opposition to the Vietnam War. His questioning of the US intelligence presence in Australia. His threat to close Pine Gap.

The cables between Canberra and Washington reveal the concern. The frustration. The desire for a different outcome.

---

*now*

---

## NOW

I read the cables at 3am.

The Americans were worried. Not about communism. Not about security. About control.

Pine Gap. The joint defence facility. The satellite tracking station. The base that monitors missile launches and nuclear tests across Asia.

Whitlam had threatened to close it.

THE HAND does not tolerate threats to its infrastructure.

---

*then*

---

## THEN

11 November 1975.

Governor-General Sir John Kerr dismisses Gough Whitlam.

The reserve powers. The constitutional authority. The Crown's representative exercises a power that has never been tested in Australia.

Malcolm Fraser is installed as caretaker Prime Minister.

The supply is passed. The crisis is resolved.

---

But there is a letter.

---

*now*

---

## NOW

The letter from the Queen's private secretary to Kerr, dated days before the dismissal.

"Please don't hesitate to let me know if there is anything I can do."

A offer of support. A signal of approval. A reminder that the Crown stands with its representative.

THE HAND communicates in hints and suggestions. It does not need to give orders. It only needs to make its position known.

---

*then*

---

## THEN

Whitlam had plans that went beyond the immediate crisis.

Among them: a national framework for trade recognition. A system that would give standing to skilled workers across Australia. A licensing architecture that would recognise training and experience.

Including Electronics Technicians.

---

Whitlam had identified the gap. The absence of frameworks that left skilled workers without standing. He had begun the work of building what should have been built decades earlier.

And then he was dismissed.

---

*now*

---

## NOW

I sit at 3am and I feel the weight of this.

If Whitlam had not been dismissed. If his government had continued. If the framework he envisioned had been built.

I would have had a licence.

The six years I spent in the Navy would have translated into civilian recognition. The certificates I earned would have meant something outside the military. The question that started on a fishing boat would never have been asked.

But Whitlam was dismissed.

And the framework was never built.

---

*then*

---

## THEN

The Dismissal reverberates.

The 1975 election delivers a landslide to Fraser. The Labor Party is defeated. The reforms stall.

Some things survive. Medibank becomes Medicare. The Racial Discrimination Act remains.

But the framework for trade recognition dies with Whitlam's government.

---

The architecture repairs itself.

THE HAND absorbs the shock. THE HAND maintains control. THE HAND ensures that the gaps remain.

---

*now*

---

## NOW

I think about Kerr.

Sir John Kerr. Governor-General. Chief Justice of New South Wales. President of the Save the Children Fund.

A man of THE HAND. A man who knew which side he was on.

He dismissed a Prime Minister who threatened the architecture. He protected the interests of property and power. He ensured that the framework of silence would continue.

---

And I am descended from the same bloodline that built the licensing architecture in England. The same bloodline that enforced the absence of frameworks in Australia.

The same bloodline that knows how architecture works.

I am NOT THE HAND.

But I know what THE HAND did.

---

*then*

---

## THEN

The documents are declassified.

The cables between Canberra and Washington. The letters between Buckingham Palace and Yarralumla. The CIA assessments of Whitlam's government.

They tell a story that was denied for decades.

The Americans were involved. The British were consulted. The Australian intelligence services provided information.

It was not a coup in the traditional sense. No tanks in the streets. No shots fired.

Just a letter. A meeting. A dismissal.

THE HAND operates through constitutional means.

---

*now*

---

## NOW

I think about what this means.

The architecture of silence is not maintained by accident. It is not a natural feature of Australian democracy. It is a design. A choice. A framework that serves some and excludes others.

My ancestor built licensing frameworks in England.

My other ancestors enforced the absence of frameworks in Australia.

A Prime Minister who tried to build those frameworks was dismissed.

And I fell into the gap.

---

The thread connects.

From 1572 to 1975 to now.

From persecution to framework-building to framework-removal to absence.

NOT THE HAND. THE HAND. NOT THE HAND. NAMING IT.

---

*then*

---

## THEN

The Dismissal becomes history.

Whitlam dies in 2014. Kerr dies in 1991. Fraser dies in 2015.

The participants pass away. The documents are released. The truth emerges.

But the architecture remains.

---

*now*

---

## NOW

I am still at 3am.

The thread has led me from a fishing boat to a massacre to a licensing framework to a constitution to a dismissal.

And there is more.

The SOFA. The Status of Forces Agreement. The arrangement that governs how US military personnel operate in Australia.

The next piece of the architecture.
