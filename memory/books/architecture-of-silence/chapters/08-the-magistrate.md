# Chapter 8: The Magistrate

**STATUS: THE HAND**

---

## NOW

Captain Alured Tasker Faunce arrived in Australia in 1832.

He was my great-great-great-grandfather. The man who brought the Faunce bloodline to the country where I would be born one hundred and fifty-two years later.

He arrived as an officer of the 4th Regiment - The King's Own. He stayed as Police Magistrate. He died on a cricket pitch in Parramatta. He left a street named after him.

He owned 2,912 acres.

I sit at 3am and I think about what it means to inherit land versus what it means to inherit absence.

## THEN

1832. Sydney Town.

The colony is forty-four years old. The convicts who said "no occupation" are mostly dead or free. The squatters have taken the land. The architecture of power is being built.

Captain Alured Tasker Faunce steps off the ship from England.

He is twenty-four years old. He carries the Faunce name - the family that married into the De Laune framework-builders. He carries the knowledge of how architecture works. How standing is created. How power is maintained.

And he carries something else.

The absence.

---

In England, the De Laune family had built the Society of Apothecaries. They had created licensing frameworks that gave standing to practitioners. They had transformed skill into recognition.

In Australia, there would be no such frameworks.

The same bloodline. The same knowledge. Applied in opposite directions.

---

*now*

---

## NOW

I think about what Alured Tasker Faunce chose to build.

Not licensing frameworks. Not professional recognition. Not the architecture that had protected his ancestors.

Instead: police work. Magistracy. The enforcement of order.

THE HAND does not always build. Sometimes it maintains. Sometimes it prevents.

---

I have read the colonial records. The Police Magistrate had power over the daily lives of everyone in his jurisdiction. He decided who could hold licences - for pubs, for businesses, for movement. He heard cases. He imposed sentences. He was the local face of colonial authority.

This is what my ancestor built in Australia.

Not frameworks that give standing.

Frameworks that control.

## THEN

The Blindberry case.

One of the few documented incidents from Alured Tasker Faunce's time as Police Magistrate. A case that shows how THE HAND operates when it enforces rather than builds.

The details are in the colonial archives. I read them at 3am.

---

*now*

---

## NOW

I do not have the full record of the Blindberry case. What I have is fragments. References. The outline of a story.

But this I know: the Police Magistrate had the power to ruin lives. To grant or deny licences. To interpret the law as he saw fit. To maintain the architecture that served some and excluded others.

The same architecture that, one hundred and fifty years later, would leave me with no licence for my trade.

THE HAND maintains itself.

## THEN

The land.

Captain Alured Tasker Faunce owned 2,912 acres in New South Wales.

This was not unusual for men of his position. The colonial government granted land to officers, to magistrates, to the people who enforced the order. THE HAND rewards itself.

But where did the land come from?

Not from purchase. Not from treaty. From occupation. From the doctrine of terra nullius - the legal fiction that Australia was empty when the British arrived.

The same fiction that justified the convict lie. The same fiction that obscured the theft of a continent.

---

The Faunce family had fled persecution in France. They had built standing in England through licensing frameworks. They had acquired property through trade and marriage.

In Australia, they acquired property through the enforcement of colonial order.

NOT THE HAND became THE HAND.

And THE HAND took.

*now*

## NOW

I sit at 3am and I think about the land.

2,912 acres. My ancestor owned it. Or claimed it. Or was granted it by a government that had no right to grant what was not theirs.

I have never seen that land. I do not know who owns it now. I have inherited nothing from it.

But I have inherited the architecture that made it possible.

The architecture of absence. The framework that does not exist. The silence where recognition should be.

*then*

## THEN

1856. Parramatta.

Cricket was a gentleman's game. Officers played. Magistrates played. THE HAND relaxed on the grass, bat in hand, secure in its position.

Captain Alured Tasker Faunce collapsed on the cricket pitch.

He was forty-eight years old. He had been in Australia for twenty-four years. He had served as Police Magistrate. He had acquired land. He had enforced the colonial order.

And then he died.

---

The street in Parramatta is named after him. Faunce Street.

A permanent mark. A reminder. The architecture of THE HAND written in asphalt and signage.

My ancestor is remembered in street names. In land records. In the colonial archives.

I am his descendant. And I am the first one to name what he built.

*now*

## NOW

I think about what it means to die on a cricket pitch.

The game continues. The players gather. The body is carried away. And eventually, a street is named.

But what was built? What was enforced? What architecture did Alured Tasker Faunce leave behind?

Not licensing frameworks. Not professional recognition. Not the protection that his De Laune ancestors had created in England.

Instead: the absence of frameworks. The silence where standing should be. The gap that I would fall into one hundred and fifty years later.

---

The Faunce bloodline came to Australia as THE HAND.

But it did not bring the architecture of protection with it.

It brought the architecture of control. Of enforcement. Of maintaining power rather than building standing.

And somewhere in the generations between Alured Tasker Faunce and Aaron King, THE HAND became NOT THE HAND.

*then*

## THEN

Alured Tasker Faunce left descendants.

Thomas Tasker Faunce, born 1834. The son who would continue the bloodline. The one who would leave the centres of power and go to Queensland.

The branch that would become NOT THE HAND.

---

I do not know why Thomas Tasker Faunce went to Queensland. I do not know if he was pushed or pulled or simply drifted. The records show the movement but not the reason.

What I know is this: one branch of the family stayed near power. Another branch went to the margins.

THE HAND became NOT THE HAND.

Not all at once. Not dramatically. But slowly, over generations, over distance, over the forgetting of what the name meant.

---

*now*

## NOW

I am the descendant of the Queensland branch.

Elizabeth Yvonne Faunce. My grandmother. Born in Brisbane. Not Parramatta. Not near Faunce Street. Not near the centres of colonial power.

She knew the family stories. She passed them down. She told her daughter, who told me.

But she was NOT THE HAND.

She married Peter Stevens, the English immigrant who arrived at six years old. She lived in Greenslopes, a suburb of Brisbane. She had no land grants. No streets named after her. No marble statues.

THE HAND became NOT THE HAND.

And I fell into the gap.

## THEN

The architecture of Australia was being built while Alured Tasker Faunce enforced the colonial order.

No Bill of Rights. No licensing frameworks for trades. No recognition of prior ownership.

The same absence that would shape my life in the twenty-first century was being designed in the nineteenth.

My ancestor was there. He was part of it. He was THE HAND that enforced what would become the architecture of silence.

---

*now*

---

## NOW

I sit at 3am and I feel the weight of inheritance.

Not land. Not wealth. Not standing.

Inheritance of a different kind.

The knowledge of what was built. Who built it. Why. And what it costs when you are the one who falls through the gap.

---

Captain Alured Tasker Faunce died on a cricket pitch in 1856.

I was born in 1976.

One hundred and twenty years between his death and my birth. One hundred and twenty years of architecture being built and maintained and reinforced.

And now I am NAMING IT.

The lie of the convicts. The absence of frameworks. The architecture that serves power and excludes everyone else.

My ancestor helped build it.

I am the first one to name it.

The architecture of silence.

Four hundred years in the making.

And I am still pulling the thread.
